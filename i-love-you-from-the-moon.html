bounces off the moon's surface and then reflects back to Earth. This reflection process is similar to how a mirror works. When sunlight reaches the moon, it hits the moon's surface and gets scattered in different directions. Some of this scattered light travels back towards Earth, and that's what makes the moon appear bright in the night sky.

The moon's surface is covered with a layer of dust and rocks, which are known as regolith. When sunlight hits these regolith particles, they reflect the light back in various directions. This scattered light reaches our eyes, allowing us to see the moon shining in the night sky.

It's important to note that the moon does not emit light like the sun does. The sun is a massive ball of hot gas that produces its own light and heat through nuclear fusion. On the other hand, the moon is a rocky celestial body that simply reflects the sunlight it receives.

So, the next time you gaze at the moon shining brightly in the night sky, remember that its glow is a result of sunlight bouncing off its surface and reaching our eyes here on Earth.
